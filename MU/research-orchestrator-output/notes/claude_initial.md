## 7. Counterarguments / Disconfirming Evidence

I'm explicitly hunting for what makes the trade wrong.

- **Cycle timing fallacy.** "AI is structural so memory is different this time" is the same argument made in every prior cycle. PC era, smartphone era, server era — each was structural and each cycle still rolled over because supply *responds*. Capex announcements in Korea through 2025 were already large; supply hits 2027.
- **HBM is not safe just because it's "AI memory."** HBM contracts are largely committed forward, but pricing is renegotiated; and the *capacity* shift toward HBM tightens commodity DRAM only until non-AI demand softens, at which point both fall. HBM is a margin lever, not a moat.
- **Samsung catching up changes the math.** If Samsung has now passed NVIDIA HBM3E qualification (a 2025–2026 watch item I cannot confirm), MU's HBM share gains thesis is weakened. The other agents must check this — it is the single most important piece of disconfirming evidence available right now.
- **The "exit by year-end" plan is calendar-based, not signal-based.** Memory cycles do not respect calendar quarters. Forcing exit by Dec 2026 may sell into strength or hold into weakness purely on date.
- **Crowdedness.** "AI memory" has been the consensus semis trade for 18+ months. Crowded thematic trades unwind violently on the first disappointment.
- **The implied $524 price is not yet verified.** If accurate, this is itself disconfirming evidence: the easy upside is likely behind us.
- **Portfolio context matters.** With 32% in FFIV and ~50% cash, the question isn't only "is MU good" but "is *adding* MU the best use of marginal capital vs. the existing concentration risk and cash drag." The brief almost prompts the investor toward stacking more semi exposure (SOXL discussed) — that path increases correlated single-factor risk.
